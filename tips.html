Tips, Safety & FAQ

🎒 Before you ride — the essentials

Get an EasyCard 悠遊卡

The tap card that unlocks almost everything — YouBike, MRT, buses and convenience stores. Buy one at any 7-Eleven, FamilyMart or MRT station (about NT$100 for the card) and top it up with cash.

Bring your passport

You won't need it on the riverside paths, but private rental shops (for road/mountain bikes) usually ask for a passport or ID as a deposit.

Carry cash

Riverside snack stalls, temples and small shops are often cash-only. Convenience stores have ATMs that accept foreign cards.

Download a few apps

YouBike (find bikes & docks), Google Maps (has a bicycle layer for Taipei), and CWA or any weather app to check rain and typhoons before you set out.

🚲 Renting a bike as a visitor

YouBike 2.0 (public share)

Docking stations are on nearly every corner and right by most riverside entrances. Tap your EasyCard on the dock post to release a bike, ride, then return it to any station and tap out. Pricing starts around NT$10 per 30 minutes — cheap for short hops.

First-time registration

An EasyCard must be registered once before first use, via a station kiosk or the YouBike app. Registration has traditionally needed a local phone number; the app now also supports credit-card payment for some visitors — check the current options at youbike.com.tw.

Rental shops (road & mountain)

For the mountain climbs or a longer road ride, a proper geared bike from a rental shop near Tamsui, Bitan or Ximen is worth it. Bring your passport; helmets and locks are usually included.

Check the bike first

Before you roll: squeeze both brakes, bounce-test the tyres, and raise the seat to hip height. If a YouBike feels off, dock it and grab another — it's free to swap.

🛑 Rules of the road & etiquette

  • Ride on the right. Taiwan drives and rides on the right-hand side.
  • Bikeway etiquette. Keep right, pass on the left, use a bell, and give way to pedestrians. Some park stretches have slow zones or "dismount and walk" signs near crowds and bridges — follow them.
  • Helmets. Not legally required for bicycles in Taiwan, but strongly recommended — especially on the fast mountain descents.
  • Lights at night. Use front and rear lights after dark; riverside paths are only partly lit.
  • Bikes on the MRT. Full-size bikes are allowed only on certain lines/stations and off-peak times, with a bike ticket — not during rush hour. Folding bikes in a bag are fine anytime. YouBikes can't go on the MRT (just dock and grab another on the far side).
  • Watch the scooters. On the road routes, scooters filter everywhere and may pass close — ride predictably and signal your turns.
  • Never ride drunk. Cycling under the influence carries real fines here.

⚠️ Safety & precautions

🌧️

River floodgates & heavy rain

The riverside bikeways sit below the flood walls and you enter through gates in the embankment. During typhoons or heavy rain these gates are closed and the paths can flood fast. Always check the forecast, and if rain is heavy, skip the river paths that day.

Heat & hydration

Summers are hot and very humid. Carry more water than you think you need, ride early morning or evening, and rest in the shade. Convenience stores (water, cold drinks, toilets) are everywhere.

Sun protection

The river paths are wide open with little shade. Sunscreen, sunglasses and a cap or arm sleeves make a long ride much more pleasant.

Mountain descents

Roads like Maokong, Balaka and Wulai drop fast with tight bends and buses. Feather both brakes, take corners wide of blind spots, and don't outrun your sightlines.

Know your exit

Most riverside parks have numbered floodgate exits back into the city and YouBike docks near them — handy if the weather turns or you tire out.

🗓️ Weather & best seasons

Best months

October–December and March–April are the sweet spots: cooler, drier and clear. Winter is mild in the city but Yangmingshan can be cold, wet and foggy up top.

Rainy & typhoon season

The "plum rains" fall in May–June, and typhoon season runs roughly July–October. Ride around the weather, not through it.

Time of day

Beat the midday heat: start at sunrise for empty paths, or ride the late afternoon into golden hour — the Tamsui sunset is worth timing for.

❓ Frequently asked questions

Do I need a licence or permit to cycle?

No. Anyone can ride a bicycle in Taiwan without a licence, and there's no age requirement for the riverside paths.

Can I rent a bike without a local phone number?

Sometimes tricky with YouBike, which has historically tied registration to a Taiwanese mobile number — though the app now offers credit-card options for visitors. The simplest fallback is a private rental shop near Tamsui, Bitan or Ximen, which rents to tourists with just a passport.

Are helmets required?

No — helmets aren't legally required for bicycles (only for scooters and motorbikes). We still recommend one, especially for the mountain descents.

Is it safe to cycle in Taipei?

The riverside bikeways are car-free and very safe — great for families and first-timers. The road routes (the mountain climbs and the coast) share the road with cars and scooters, so they need more experience and care. Every route on this site is labelled 🚲 Bikeway or 🚗 Road route so you know which is which.

Can I take my bike on the metro (MRT)?

Full-size bikes only on certain lines/stations and off-peak times, with a separate bike ticket — never during rush hour. Folding bikes packed in a bag are allowed anytime. YouBikes aren't allowed on the MRT; just dock and pick up a new one after your metro ride.

What happens if it rains or there's a typhoon?

The riverside paths sit below the flood walls and their gates close in heavy rain, when the paths can flood. Check the forecast and, if it's stormy, choose a city ride or sightseeing instead. Never ride onto a flooding river path.

How much does it cost to ride?

YouBike is roughly NT$10 per 30 minutes for the first hours, paid by EasyCard. Rental-shop bikes typically run a few hundred NT dollars per day.

Where do I find water, toilets and food?

Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart) are on almost every block and have toilets, drinks and snacks. The riverside parks also have public restrooms, water fountains and, on weekends, food stalls.
